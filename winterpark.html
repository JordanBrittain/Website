A project in which I designed a potential product for Winter Park Ski Resort. This page will take you through the project from the initial research and design stages all the way to the visual identity.

The Client

The Challenge

For over 70 years, Winter Park Ski Resort has retained its reputation as one of the best ski resorts in the Rockies. Located just 67 miles northwest of Denver, Winter Park Resort is the closest major destination resort to Denver International Airport. Vacationers and natives alike retreat to this haven during the winter months to hit the slopes. Winter Park’s tireless effort to provide a top-notch experience keeps their customers coming back.

How can technology be leveraged in order to improve the Winter Park experience?

Conditions

Weather

The amenities of the park are constantly opening and closing for a variety of reasons. For example, snowstorms may cause a lift to shutdown and reopen multiple times during a day. It’s not uncommon that a person will ski over to an amenity just to find that it’s closed. These all too common incidents prove to be inconvenient and frustrating.

When it comes to the slopes, weather is a huge factor. Heading out with too little or too much clothing can prove to be a miserable and sometimes even dangerous experience. Weather on the mountains is also often unpredictable, with storms blowing in at a moments notice. These storms shut down lifts and sometimes even evacuate the mountain. Although, it’s not just about comfort and safety. The amount of snowfall also greatly determines the quality of the slopes.

At a glance, people are able to retrieve info on all of the resort’s amenities from the runs, to the lifts, to the restaurants.

The feed helps users stay up to date on the status of these amenities.

The most essential weather information like snowfall and forecast is offered on both an hourly and daily view.

This same information is also offered in the watch app.

Social

People have the ability to create “groups” which help them stay in touch throughout the day.

The group feed not only lets users communicate with each other, but also keeps them up to date on each others activity such as the lifts they’re getting on and the amenities they’re checking in at.

For most, snow sports are a social activity. People go with their friends, spouses, clubs, bachelor parties, etc. However, due to the nature of snow sports it can be challenging to keep the group together. Crashing and getting left behind, taking the wrong run and getting lost, and accidentally getting on the wrong lift are all common occurrences.

The interactive map helps users discover and navigate to all that Winter Park has to offer.

Users can navigate on-the-fly using the watch app.

Discovery

How would this start?

One of the things that differentiates Winter Park from its competitors is the quantity and quality of it’s infrastructure. The resort is huge, with over 3,000 ski-able acres and 20+ bars and restaurants on the mountain. You can ski for an entire day and only see a fraction of what the park has to offer.

It wouldn’t be feasible to build all of this functionality into the initial product, so where would it make the most sense to start? Budgets, timelines, and a number of other factors would dictate the answer to this question, but I thought I’d throw in my two cents from a design perspective.

 

I think it would make the most sense to start with the “conditions” functionality. Through the research and analysis stages it was found that this sort of functionality was the most useful and sought after. Secondly, Winter Park already keeps track of its facilities statuses in real time, so the infrastructure needed to make a lot of these features possible is already in place. The other functionality could then be rolled out after the initial product gained traction.

Part 03

Identity

The identity I developed for the product aims to retain the equity in the parks current identity but push it further in order to create a little more visual interest, all while putting usability first.

Animation

Animations are smooth and fluid echoing skiing itself, but are still quick enough to be functional.

 

For nerds: Most elements are animated with a one second cubic ease out.

 

The typeforms in Montserrat balance humanist characteristics with strict geometry. This geometry echoes the other visual elements to create a cohesive tone. Additionally, the family is diverse, having several different weights and styles that are readable at small sizes and pack a punch at larger display sizes.

The large swaths of white give the information room to breath and carry the feeling of the slopes themselves while the vibrant colors and geometric shapes were derived from the current way-finding used at the resort, conceptually connecting the application to the existing system.

 

Type

Color

The interactive elements of the application are indicated with a vibrant orange, helping users identify functionality.
